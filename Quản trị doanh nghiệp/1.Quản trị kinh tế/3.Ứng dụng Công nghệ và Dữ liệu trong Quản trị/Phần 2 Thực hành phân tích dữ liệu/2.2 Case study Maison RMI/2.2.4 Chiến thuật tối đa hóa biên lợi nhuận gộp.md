### Tối Đa Hóa Biên Lợi Nhuận Gộp — Gross Margin Strategy

Gross Margin trong premium F&B là fundamentally different từ mass-market F&B. Premium brand có room to charge more — nhưng cũng có higher cost expectations (quality ingredients, skilled preparation). Net gross margin is result of managing both sides well.

> **Bài học:** Gross Margin optimization không chỉ là cost reduction — là value creation through better product mix, smarter procurement, reduced waste, và pricing confidence. A premium brand that discounts aggressively destroys more gross margin than any procurement savings can recover.

---

**F&B Gross Margin Structure:**

```
Revenue:                    100%
Food & Beverage Cost:      -28%  (target for premium F&B)
Labour (direct):           -22%
Gross Contribution:         50%

Fixed Costs:
  Rent:                    -18%
  Utilities:                -5%
  Other ops:                -5%
Operating Contribution:     22%

This is "restaurant-level EBITDA" — before central costs allocated
Corporate overhead, marketing, tech:  -8%
Location EBITDA:                       14%
```

---

**Menu Engineering — the gross margin toolkit:**

```
4-quadrant analysis (Stars, Plowhorses, Puzzles, Dogs):

HIGH MARGIN + HIGH POPULARITY: Stars
  → Keep on menu; feature prominently; protect quality
  → Signature items that define brand

HIGH MARGIN + LOW POPULARITY: Puzzles
  → Investigate why: wrong placement? Wrong name? Wrong price point?
  → If fixable: rename, reposition, promote
  → If not: replace

LOW MARGIN + HIGH POPULARITY: Plowhorses
  → Volume drivers; can't remove without customer backlash
  → Optimize cost (ingredient substitution if quality maintained)
  → Price increase test (small increment; measure volume impact)

LOW MARGIN + LOW POPULARITY: Dogs
  → Remove from menu
  → Simplification reduces kitchen complexity and waste
```

---

**Beverage vs Food margin — the key insight for F&B:**

```
Typical F&B Gross Margins:

BEVERAGES:
  Coffee (espresso-based): 65–75% gross margin
  Juice, smoothies: 55–65% gross margin
  Alcoholic beverages (cocktails): 70–80% gross margin
  Soft drinks: 60–70% gross margin

FOOD:
  Pastries (outsourced): 45–55% gross margin
  Made-in-kitchen dishes: 55–65% gross margin
  Premium protein dishes: 45–55% gross margin

Implication for Maison RMI:
  Menu mix that pushes beverage-heavy vs food-heavy → higher gross margin
  "All-day cafe" model (beverage-driven) typically higher gross margin
  than "restaurant" model (food-driven)
```

**Procurement Strategy — reducing F&B cost:**

```
Strategic procurement for premium F&B:

1. Supplier consolidation:
   → Fewer suppliers, more volume per supplier → better pricing
   → Relationship depth → preferential treatment in shortage

2. Contract pricing for key ingredients:
   → Fix coffee bean prices quarterly → reduce commodity volatility
   → Long-term supplier agreements with price escalation caps

3. Central kitchen leverage:
   → Bulk purchase raw materials
   → Process centrally (reduce per-location labour cost)
   → Consistent portion control → reduce waste and cost variability

4. Seasonal menu adjustment:
   → Feature ingredients when cheapest and freshest
   → Reduces F&B cost while improving product quality
   → Customers appreciate seasonality as "premium" attribute

5. Waste tracking and reduction:
   → Target: food waste <3% of F&B cost
   → Track by item, by location, by time of day
   → Root cause: overproduction, expiry, preparation waste
   → FIFO (First In First Out) discipline in all stock rooms
```

---

**Pricing Discipline — the most critical gross margin lever:**

```
Premium brand pricing principles:

1. Never compete on price with mass market:
   → Price signal is a quality signal in premium positioning
   → Price cuts → send wrong signal → brand dilution
   → If customer converts on price: wrong customer for brand

2. Price increase strategy:
   → Small, regular adjustments (5–8% annually) less visible than large jumps
   → Increase during menu refresh (natural context for pricing change)
   → Prioritize high-volume items for small % increase (big revenue impact)
   → Test with subset before full rollout

3. Bundle pricing and upsell:
   → Combo meals: reduce perceived price while increasing average check
   → "Add on" culture: training staff to offer complementary items
   → Membership/loyalty: members pay upfront, reducing per-transaction friction

4. Menu price presentation:
   → Remove currency symbol on premium menus (reduces price salience)
   → Anchor pricing: premium option makes mid-tier look reasonable
   → Decoy pricing: three-tier structure (small/medium/large) → guide to medium
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Danny Meyer's "Setting the Table" concept applied to premium F&B: "the price you charge reflects your confidence in what you deliver." Premium brands that discount undermine their own value proposition. Research on luxury goods: demand is often inelastic or even positively correlated with price in premium segments (Veblen goods) — a price increase signals exclusivity and desirability. This doesn't mean arbitrary increases, but it does mean pricing should reflect delivered value, not market floor.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Optimize gross margin by cutting ingredient quality. "We can save 2% on F&B cost if we use lower-quality coffee beans." In premium F&B, quality IS the product. Customers paying premium are buying an experience — and they can taste the difference. Cost-cutting that reduces product quality is margin-positive short-term and brand-destroying long-term.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Ignore portion size in cost calculation. Standardized portion sizes are critical for both cost control and customer experience consistency. Without portion standardization: cost varies unpredictably across batches and staff; customers get different value for same price. Investment in proper measuring equipment, scales, and training pays back quickly.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Pursue gross margin without considering throughput. A slow, high-margin item that occupies table time can be less profitable than a fast, slightly-lower-margin item. Revenue per available seat hour (RevPASH) = Revenue / (Seats × Operating Hours). Optimize for RevPASH, not just gross margin per item.
