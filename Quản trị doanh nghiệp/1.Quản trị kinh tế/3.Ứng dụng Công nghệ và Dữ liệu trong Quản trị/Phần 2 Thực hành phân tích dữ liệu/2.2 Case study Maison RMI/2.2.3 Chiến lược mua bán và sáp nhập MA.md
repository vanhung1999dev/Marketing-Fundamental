### Chiến Lược M&A — Mua Bán và Sáp Nhập Trong F&B

M&A (Mergers & Acquisitions) là một trong những growth levers của Maison RMI — acquiring brands, capabilities, hoặc market position nhanh hơn organic growth. Trong F&B luxury, M&A có thể mean acquiring an international brand license, a competitor, hoặc a complementary brand.

> **Bài học:** M&A trong F&B không phải chỉ là mua financial asset — là mua brand equity, customer relationships, và human capital. Khi thương vụ fails, thường không phải vì financial model sai — mà vì cultural integration, talent retention, hoặc operational complexity được underestimated.

---

**Tại sao F&B Companies thực hiện M&A:**

```
Strategic rationales:
  1. Brand acquisition:
     "Faster than building from scratch"
     Buy established brand with existing customers, real estate, and team
     
  2. Geographic expansion:
     Acquire local player với established locations in new market
     Avoid the challenges of building from zero in unfamiliar geography
  
  3. Capability acquisition:
     Technology, central kitchen capacity, supply chain relationships
     Particularly relevant: acquire food tech or delivery platform capability
  
  4. Portfolio diversification:
     Add adjacent category (e.g., F&B brand adding lifestyle retail)
     Reduce dependence on single brand/format
  
  5. International brand licensing:
     Acquire rights to operate well-known international brand in VN
     Benefit: brand recognition; Risk: royalties, strict brand standards
```

**M&A Valuation Framework for F&B:**

```
Common valuation approaches:

1. EV/EBITDA Multiple:
   Enterprise Value = EBITDA × Industry Multiple
   F&B multiple: 8–15× EBITDA (premium brands higher end)
   
   Example:
     Acquisition target annual EBITDA: 20 tỷ
     EV/EBITDA multiple: 10×
     Enterprise Value = 200 tỷ
     Less: Net Debt = -30 tỷ
     Equity Value = 170 tỷ

2. Revenue Multiple (early-stage, pre-profit):
   EV/Revenue: 0.5–2.0× (depends on growth rate and margin potential)

3. EBITDA × Multiple + Location Portfolio Value:
   Particularly for restaurant chains:
   EBITDA multiple + premium for real estate options/lease rights

4. DCF (Discounted Cash Flow):
   Project FCF for 5–7 years + Terminal Value
   Discount at WACC (including acquisition risk premium)
```

**Due Diligence — critical areas for F&B:**

```
Financial Due Diligence:
  □ Historical P&L by location (not just consolidated)
  □ Real lease terms: escalation clauses, expiry dates, renewal options
  □ Related party transactions (common in family-owned F&B)
  □ Revenue recognition: recognizing gift cards, membership deposits
  □ Supplier terms and commitments (exclusivity? pricing?)

Operational Due Diligence:
  □ Quality consistency across locations (mystery shop all locations)
  □ Key person dependency: does the brand = one chef or owner?
  □ Standard operating procedures: documented or in people's heads?
  □ Real food cost and waste rates (often underreported pre-acquisition)

Brand Due Diligence:
  □ Social media sentiment and review scores (Google, Facebook)
  □ Customer loyalty: are they loyal to brand or to specific staff?
  □ Brand legal protection: trademarks registered? Any IP disputes?

HR Due Diligence:
  □ Key talent retention: who will stay? Who might leave?
  □ Employment contracts: any change-of-control clauses?
  □ BHXH and tax compliance: underpayment is a common liability
```

**Post-Merger Integration (PMI) — where deals succeed or fail:**

```
100-day integration plan:
  Day 1–30: Stabilize
    □ Communicate clearly to all staff: what's changing, what's not
    □ Keep founding team engaged (if staying)
    □ Don't change customer-facing elements yet
    □ Understand all operational dependencies
  
  Day 31–60: Integrate
    □ Integrate financial reporting and accounting
    □ Consolidate procurement where leverage exists
    □ Identify quick-win operational improvements
    □ Begin culture integration (values, ways of working)
  
  Day 61–100: Optimize
    □ Implement shared services where beneficial
    □ Begin brand portfolio coordination
    □ Identify synergies achieved and remaining
    □ Assess key talent retention
```

**Synergy Analysis — what's real vs wishful:**

```
Types of M&A synergies:

Revenue synergies (harder to achieve, longer time):
  → Cross-selling between brand customer bases
  → New market access from acquired distribution
  → Brand portfolio bundling

Cost synergies (more achievable, faster):
  → Procurement leverage: negotiate better with consolidated volume
  → Shared central kitchen: reduce per-location food cost
  → Shared back-office: one finance/HR team for multiple brands
  → Technology cost sharing: one ERP, POS, CRM for all brands

Common mistake: Count 100% of synergies; realize 50%
Better approach: Model synergies conservatively; value deal on standalone basis;
               treat synergies as upside to be earned
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Research by McKinsey on M&A: 60–70% of acquisitions destroy value for acquirers. Primary reasons: (1) overpaying — too much synergy optimism baked into price; (2) culture clash — acquirer imposes its culture, key talent from target leaves; (3) integration complexity underestimated — two IT systems, two teams, two ways of working. The deals that create value most consistently: bolt-on acquisitions of smaller companies in same sector where acquirer has clear operational expertise.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Rush to rebrand immediately after acquisition. "Now that we own the brand, let's update it to match our standards." Existing customers of the acquired brand value what they already know. Premature rebranding → customer confusion → revenue decline. Allow the brand to run independently for 6–12 months, understand its customer base, then evolve gradually.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Underestimate technology integration cost. Two different POS systems, two loyalty programs, two supply chain setups — converging these takes 12–24 months and significant IT investment. Include full integration cost in acquisition budget, not just deal cost.

> **Cạm bẫy:** "Strategic" justification for overpriced deal. "We need this brand to complete our portfolio." When strategic imperative drives price, discipline breaks down. Acquisition at too high a multiple means years of earnings must pay back the premium — limiting investment in actual growth. Discipline: set a walk-away price before negotiations and stick to it.
