### Tối Ưu Chu Kỳ Đặt Hàng Theo Dữ Liệu Thực Tế

EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) và chu kỳ đặt hàng tối ưu trả lời câu hỏi: "Đặt hàng bao nhiêu mỗi lần, và đặt hàng bao lâu một lần?" Đây là bài toán cân bằng giữa chi phí đặt hàng (giảm khi mua lượng lớn ít lần) và chi phí lưu kho (tăng khi mua lượng lớn ít lần).

> **Bài học:** EOQ là điểm tối ưu lý thuyết — nhưng giá trị thực của nó không phải ở con số cụ thể mà ở framework tư duy: mọi quyết định "đặt bao nhiêu, đặt khi nào" đều có hai đánh đổi ẩn. Bỏ qua một trong hai (chỉ tối thiểu chi phí đặt hàng hoặc chỉ tối thiểu chi phí lưu kho) luôn tạo ra chi phí ẩn.

---

**EOQ — Economic Order Quantity:**

```
EOQ Formula:
  EOQ = √(2DS/H)
  
  Where:
    D = Annual demand (units)
    S = Ordering cost per order (triệu VNĐ)
    H = Annual holding cost per unit (= unit cost × carrying rate)

Example:
  Product: Raw material for manufacturing
  Annual demand (D): 12,000 units
  Unit cost: 100,000 VNĐ
  Carrying cost rate: 25%/year
  Holding cost per unit (H): 100,000 × 25% = 25,000 VNĐ/year
  Ordering cost (S): 2,000,000 VNĐ/order (logistics, admin, receiving)
  
  EOQ = √(2 × 12,000 × 2,000,000 / 25,000)
       = √(48,000,000,000 / 25,000)
       = √1,920,000
       = 1,386 units per order

Optimal order frequency:
  Orders per year = D / EOQ = 12,000 / 1,386 = 8.7 → 9 orders/year
  Order cycle = 12 months / 9 = every 1.33 months ≈ every 5.3 weeks

Comparison scenarios:
  4 orders/year (quarterly): Average inventory = 12,000/(2×4) = 1,500 units
    Holding cost: 1,500 × 25,000 = 37.5 triệu; Ordering cost: 4 × 2M = 8M; Total: 45.5M
  EOQ (9 orders/year): Average inventory = 1,386/2 = 693 units
    Holding cost: 693 × 25,000 = 17.3 triệu; Ordering cost: 9 × 2M = 18M; Total: 35.3M
  24 orders/year (bi-weekly): Average inventory = 250 units
    Holding cost: 250 × 25,000 = 6.25 triệu; Ordering cost: 24 × 2M = 48M; Total: 54.25M
  
  EOQ minimizes total cost: 35.3 triệu vs 45.5 triệu (quarterly) or 54.25 triệu (bi-weekly)
```

**EOQ Modifications for Real-World Constraints:**

```
1. MINIMUM ORDER QUANTITY (MOQ) from supplier:
   If supplier MOQ = 2,000 units but EOQ = 1,386 units:
   → Order 2,000 units (accept higher inventory for supplier compliance)
   → Negotiate MOQ reduction if volume justifies it

2. VOLUME DISCOUNTS:
   Supplier offers: <1,000 units = 100,000 VNĐ; ≥2,000 units = 92,000 VNĐ (8% discount)
   Calculate: Does discount savings exceed extra holding cost?
   Extra holding cost for 2,000 vs 1,386 units: (614 extra units × 92,000 × 25% / 2) = 7.1M
   Discount saving on 2,000 units: 2,000 × 8,000 = 16M
   → Take the discount: 16M saving > 7.1M extra cost

3. STORAGE CAPACITY CONSTRAINT:
   EOQ = 1,386 but warehouse only holds 800 units
   → Order 800 units per order; increase frequency to compensate
   → This increases total cost; justify investment in more storage if demand warrants

4. PERISHABLE GOODS:
   EOQ assumes unlimited shelf life; doesn't apply to short-shelf-life products
   → For perishables: order only what you can sell within shelf life
   → Order daily or weekly based on demand forecast; not based on cost optimization
```

**Just-In-Time (JIT) vs EOQ:**

```
JIT Philosophy: Receive inventory exactly when needed → zero inventory holding
EOQ Philosophy: Balance ordering and holding costs → optimal batch size

When JIT works better than EOQ:
  Highly reliable suppliers with short, consistent lead times
  Stable, predictable demand
  Strong supplier relationships (partnership; not transactional)
  High carrying cost products (expensive, perishable, high obsolescence)
  
  Example: Toyota production → parts delivered multiple times per day by JIT suppliers
  → Zero inventory, zero holding cost, zero space

When EOQ works better than JIT:
  Less reliable suppliers; variable lead times
  Higher ordering cost (distant suppliers, import/customs complexity)
  Volatile demand
  Lower carrying cost products
  
VN SME context:
  Full JIT rarely feasible: supplier reliability is often lower; imports require buffers
  Modified JIT: reduce order sizes toward EOQ; improve supplier reliability over time
  → Progressive path: quarterly orders → EOQ frequency → modified JIT
```

**Practical Order Cycle Management:**

```
Monthly Purchase Planning Process:

Step 1: Forecast demand for next 8 weeks (2× lead time buffer)
  Use sales history + seasonality + known pipeline
  Accuracy: 10% error acceptable for planning purposes

Step 2: Calculate stock position
  Available stock = On-hand + On-order - Committed to customers

Step 3: Identify replenishment needs
  Available stock vs (Demand forecast + Safety stock target)
  Gap = replenishment order quantity needed

Step 4: Apply EOQ/batch constraints
  Round up to EOQ or MOQ
  Check for supplier-specific constraints (lead time, minimum)

Step 5: Issue Purchase Orders
  All POs with due dates based on lead time
  Track status: ordered → in transit → received → put away

Step 6: Receive and reconcile
  3-way match: PO + Delivery Note + Invoice
  Update inventory records immediately upon receipt

Review cycle:
  A-items: weekly review; continuous replenishment
  B-items: bi-weekly review
  C-items: monthly review; batch replenishment
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** In VN's manufacturing sector, companies importing from China face a unique EOQ challenge: large MOQs, 30–45 day shipping lead times, and limited storage. The result: many companies order quarterly or semi-annually, creating very high average inventory relative to sales. The solution often lies not in applying EOQ mechanically, but in qualifying domestic or ASEAN suppliers for commodity components (shorter lead times, lower MOQ) while reserving China-sourced imports for specialty items where the price advantage justifies the inventory cost.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Treat ordering cost as fixed when it's actually variable. "Our ordering cost is 2 triệu/order." But if ordering becomes cheaper (EDI integration with supplier, automated PO generation), ordering cost drops → optimal order quantity decreases → smaller, more frequent orders → lower average inventory. As digitization reduces ordering cost, optimal order quantity should be recalculated.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Apply EOQ without considering demand variability within the cycle. EOQ calculates optimal average order size — but if demand is highly variable week-to-week, the average is misleading. EOQ works best for stable demand; for volatile demand, add safety stock buffer and use shorter review cycles.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Purchase to fill warehouse rather than purchase to meet demand. "We have warehouse space; let's buy ahead to fill it." Warehouse capacity is not a reason to buy more. Buy what demand requires + safety stock. Unused warehouse space is an opportunity to reduce warehouse footprint and cost — not an invitation to fill with excess inventory.
