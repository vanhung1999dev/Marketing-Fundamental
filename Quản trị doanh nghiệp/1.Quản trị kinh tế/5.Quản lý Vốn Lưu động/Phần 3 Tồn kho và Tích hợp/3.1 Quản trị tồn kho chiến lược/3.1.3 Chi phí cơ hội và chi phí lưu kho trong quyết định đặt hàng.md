### Chi Phí Cơ Hội và Chi Phí Lưu Kho Trong Quyết Định Đặt Hàng

Quyết định đặt hàng không chỉ là "đặt bao nhiêu" và "khi nào" — còn là hiểu đầy đủ tất cả các chi phí thực sự gắn với từng đơn vị tồn kho. Chi phí lưu kho thường bị underestimate; chi phí cơ hội thường bị bỏ qua hoàn toàn.

> **Bài học:** Giá nhập kho là chi phí rõ ràng nhất — nhưng thường chỉ là 50–60% tổng chi phí thực của hàng tồn kho. Chi phí lưu kho, rủi ro lỗi thời, và chi phí cơ hội vốn cộng lại thường bằng 20–35% giá trị hàng mỗi năm. Không tính những chi phí ẩn này → quyết định đặt hàng bị sai lệch.

---

**Toàn Bộ Chi Phí Lưu Kho — Total Carrying Cost:**

```
Annual Carrying Cost Components:

1. CAPITAL COST (chi phí vốn):
   = Inventory value × WACC (hoặc borrowing rate)
   Thường 10–15% giá trị hàng/năm
   Lý do: Tiền đang nằm trong hàng thay vì được đầu tư hoặc trả nợ

2. STORAGE COST (chi phí kho bãi):
   Warehouse rent: thường 1–3% giá trị hàng/năm
   Handling (bốc xếp, quản lý): 1–2%
   Security, utilities: 0.5–1%
   
   NOTE: For companies with excess warehouse space, marginal storage cost is low
         (rent already paid); marginal carrying cost = mainly capital cost

3. INSURANCE:
   Typically 0.5–1% của giá trị hàng/năm
   Higher for high-value, high-theft-risk, or perishable items

4. OBSOLESCENCE AND SPOILAGE RISK:
   FMCG (short shelf life): 3–5% annual write-off expected
   Fashion/seasonal: 5–10% markdown/write-off each season
   Electronics: 5–10% (rapid price erosion)
   Industrial parts: 1–2% (slow but steady obsolescence)
   Chemicals: variable based on stability

5. ADMINISTRATIVE COST:
   Cycle counting, auditing, reporting: 0.5–1%
   System cost allocated: 0.2–0.5%

TOTAL CARRYING COST RATE:
  Low-risk commodity: 18–22% per year
  Standard goods: 23–27% per year
  High-obsolescence/perishable: 30–40% per year
```

**Chi Phí Cơ Hội — Opportunity Cost:**

```
Opportunity cost = Return foregone by having capital in inventory

Most common benchmark: 
  Opportunity cost rate = WACC or bank borrowing rate (typically 10–15% in VN)
  
  This means: each 1 tỷ in inventory = 100–150 triệu/year of opportunity cost

Opportunity cost in decision-making:

SCENARIO: Supplier offers "buy 6 months now, save 5%"
  Normal purchase: buy monthly, 1 tỷ/month = 12 tỷ/year
  Bulk purchase: buy 6 months = 6 tỷ upfront
  
  Saving: 5% × 6 tỷ = 300 triệu
  Extra inventory carrying (5 extra months at 25% rate):
    Average extra inventory = 6 tỷ / 2 = 3 tỷ (average extra holding)
    Duration: 5 months
    Carrying cost: 3 tỷ × 25% × 5/12 = 312.5 triệu
  
  Net benefit: 300 - 312.5 = -12.5 triệu → DON'T take the bulk deal
  
  At what discount % does bulk deal make sense?
  Need saving > 312.5 triệu → on 6 tỷ purchase: need > 5.2% discount
  → Only take bulk deal if supplier offers >5.2% discount on 6-month quantity

PRACTICAL RULE:
  Bulk purchase discount justified IF:
  Discount % > Carrying cost rate × Extra holding period (in years)
  5% discount on 6 extra months: 5% > 25% × 6/12 = 12.5% → NOT justified
  5% discount on 2 extra months: 5% > 25% × 2/12 = 4.2% → JUSTIFIED
```

**Make-or-Buy — Inventory Implications:**

```
Outsourcing vs in-house production has working capital implications:

IN-HOUSE PRODUCTION:
  Carries: Raw materials + WIP + finished goods = full production chain inventory
  WC intensity: High (typically 30–50% of revenue)
  Cash cycle: Long (materials → production → FG → sale → collection)

OUTSOURCING / BUYING:
  Carries: Finished goods only (no raw materials, no WIP)
  WC intensity: Lower (10–25% of revenue)
  Cash cycle: Shorter (buy FG → sell → collect)
  
  Trade-off: Pay higher COGS (contractor margin included) vs hold less WC
  
  WC saving from outsourcing:
  In-house inventory: Raw materials (15 days) + WIP (20 days) + FG (30 days) = 65 days
  Outsourced inventory: FG (30 days) = 30 days
  Saving: 35 days of COGS = COGS/365 × 35 days
  At COGS 50 tỷ: saving = 4.8 tỷ in WC
  At 12% cost of capital: saving = 575 triệu/year in financing cost
  
  This saving partially offsets the premium paid to outsourcer
```

**Total Cost of Ownership — Decision Framework:**

```
When evaluating inventory-related decisions, always include:

Purchase Decision Template:
  Unit purchase price: ______ VNĐ
  + Transport and import cost: ______
  + Customs/duty: ______
  = Landed cost: ______
  
  + Annual carrying cost (25% of landed cost): ______
  + Average inventory days × daily carrying cost: ______
  + Obsolescence risk provision: ______
  = Total cost of holding 1 year supply
  
  Compare against:
    Alternative supplier landed cost (different terms/location)
    Outsourcing/buying finished product
    Just-in-time arrangement (lower inventory; higher per-unit cost)
  
  Decision: lowest total cost of ownership, not just lowest unit price
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** The "iceberg" of inventory costs: unit purchase price is the visible tip. Below the waterline: capital cost, storage, handling, insurance, obsolescence, and administrative costs — often 20–35% of unit price per year. Companies that compete on procurement price alone (negotiating last dollar off unit price) while ignoring carrying costs often hold too much inventory, destroying the price savings through high carrying costs. True procurement optimization includes the full iceberg, not just the tip.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Compare suppliers on unit price without including lead time and variability differences. Supplier A: 95,000 VNĐ/unit, 30-day lead time, ±5 days variability. Supplier B: 100,000 VNĐ/unit, 7-day lead time, ±1 day variability. On unit price, A wins. But B's shorter, more reliable lead time requires less safety stock. Calculate total cost including safety stock capital cost — B might win on total cost despite higher unit price.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Treat warehouse space as "free" because rent is already paid. Fixed costs are "sunk" but marginal costs still matter. More inventory → more handling labor, more stock counting time, more insurance, more chance of damage. Even in owned/already-rented warehouse, more inventory has real marginal cost. Never assume "free storage."

> **Cạm bẫy:** Justify large inventory purchases based on price protection ("prices will go up"). This is speculation, not inventory management. Unless there is strong contractual evidence of price increase AND carrying cost < expected price increase, buying ahead "just in case prices rise" is a bet, not a plan. Stick to EOQ/demand-based purchasing; let treasury handle commodity price hedging as a separate decision.
