### Vòng Quay Tồn Kho và Chi Phí Cơ Hội Vốn

DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding) đo lường thời gian trung bình hàng tồn kho nằm trong kho trước khi được bán. Mỗi ngày hàng nằm kho là một ngày vốn bị khóa — không sinh lời và mang chi phí lưu kho. Tối ưu DIO là cân bằng giữa service level (tránh hết hàng) và vốn hiệu quả (tránh dư thừa).

> **Bài học:** Tồn kho là vốn ngủ. Mỗi tỷ hàng nằm trong kho = 1 tỷ không thể tái đầu tư vào cơ hội khác. Chi phí thực của tồn kho không chỉ là tiền kho + bảo hiểm + hao hụt — còn là chi phí cơ hội của vốn bị khóa. Công ty nào hiểu và quản lý chi phí cơ hội tồn kho sẽ có lợi thế vốn có tính tích lũy.

---

**DIO — Chỉ Số Cốt Lõi:**

```
DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding) = (Inventory Balance / COGS) × 365

Example:
  Inventory Balance: 12 tỷ
  Annual COGS: 72 tỷ
  DIO = (12 / 72) × 365 = 60.8 days ≈ 61 days

Interpretation:
  Goods sit in inventory for average 61 days before being sold
  
  Industry benchmarks (VN):
    FMCG distribution: 20–35 days (fast-moving)
    F&B manufacturer: 30–50 days
    Fashion retail: 60–90 days (seasonal)
    Industrial equipment: 90–180 days
    Construction materials: 45–75 days
    Electronics: 30–45 days (fast obsolescence risk)

Inventory Turnover = COGS / Inventory = 365 / DIO
  DIO = 61: Inventory turns 6 times/year (365/61 = 5.98)
  Higher turnover = more efficient; lower = more capital tied up
```

---

**Chi Phí Toàn Phần Của Tồn Kho:**

```
Total Inventory Carrying Cost = typically 20–35% of inventory value per year

Components:
  Capital cost (opportunity cost): 10–15% (WACC or bank rate)
  Warehouse rent: 2–4%
  Insurance: 0.5–1%
  Handling (labor, equipment): 1–3%
  Obsolescence/spoilage risk: 1–5% (higher for perishable, fashion, electronics)
  Administrative: 0.5–1%
  TOTAL: ~15–30% per year

Example calculation:
  Average inventory value: 12 tỷ
  Carrying cost rate: 25%
  Annual carrying cost = 12 tỷ × 25% = 3 tỷ/year
  Monthly carrying cost = 250 triệu/month
  
  Every 1 tỷ reduction in average inventory saves 250 triệu/year in carrying cost
  → Strong incentive to reduce excess inventory
```

**Phân Tích ABC — Phân Loại Tồn Kho:**

```
ABC Classification by Revenue/COGS Contribution:

  Category A (top 20% SKU → 80% revenue): HIGH priority management
    Policy: Tight reorder points; frequent review; never stock out
    Target: Low days of supply (15–20 days)
    Monitor: Weekly
  
  Category B (next 30% SKU → 15% revenue): MEDIUM priority
    Policy: Standard reorder points; automatic replenishment
    Target: Moderate days of supply (30–45 days)
    Monitor: Bi-weekly
  
  Category C (bottom 50% SKU → 5% revenue): LOW priority
    Policy: Review for elimination or minimal stock
    Target: High days of supply acceptable OR just-in-time
    Monitor: Monthly
    Question: Should these SKUs even exist?

Action for each category:
  A items: Invest in demand forecasting accuracy; minimize stockout
  B items: Automate replenishment; periodic review
  C items: Rationalize SKU count; reduce or eliminate slow movers
```

**Chi Phí Cơ Hội — Tính Toán Thực Tế:**

```
Scenario: Company considering reducing DIO from 61 to 45 days

Current state:
  COGS: 72 tỷ/year
  DIO: 61 days
  Average Inventory: 72/365 × 61 = 12.0 tỷ
  Carrying cost (25%): 3.0 tỷ/year

Target state:
  DIO: 45 days
  Average Inventory: 72/365 × 45 = 8.88 tỷ
  Carrying cost (25%): 2.22 tỷ/year

Savings:
  Cash released: 12.0 - 8.88 = 3.12 tỷ (one-time, can repay bank debt)
  Annual carrying cost saving: 3.0 - 2.22 = 0.78 tỷ/year

What can 3.12 tỷ do?
  Repay bank loan at 10%: save 312 triệu/year in interest
  Invest in marketing: assume 5× ROI = 15.6 tỷ additional revenue
  Expand to new channel: fund initial working capital for new business unit
  → Reducing DIO is not just cost saving — it's strategic capital reallocation
```

**Nguyên Nhân Và Giải Pháp DIO Cao:**

```
Root Cause                  | Solution
----------------------------|----------------------------------------------
Poor demand forecasting     | Improve forecast accuracy; use time series
Too many SKUs               | SKU rationalization; eliminate slow movers
Long supplier lead time     | Qualify second suppliers; negotiate shorter lead times
Minimum order quantities    | Negotiate MOQ reduction; consolidate orders
Seasonal buying patterns    | Plan seasonal purchases more precisely
Fear of stockout            | Calculate optimal safety stock (data-driven, not fear-driven)
Lack of visibility          | Implement inventory management system (Kiot Viet, MISA)
Promotional over-buying     | Limit promotional purchase to projected demand + 20%
Quality issues              | Reduce returns/rework that inflate inventory
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Toyota's TPS (Toyota Production System) transformed manufacturing globally by demonstrating that low inventory is not just efficient — it's a quality and problem-solving mechanism. High inventory hides problems: when a defect is produced, it can sit in inventory for weeks before discovery (and causing cascading defects). Low inventory surfaces problems immediately. This is the deep insight behind "Just-in-Time" — not just reducing inventory cost, but using low inventory as a forcing function for quality and process excellence.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Cut inventory targets without fixing the supply chain first. If lead time from supplier is 45 days and you cut safety stock to 10 days of supply, you'll have frequent stockouts. Inventory reduction must be sequenced: first reduce lead times → then reduce safety stock. Attack the root cause (lead time uncertainty), not the symptom (high inventory).

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Measure inventory in value only, not days of supply. "Inventory is 12 tỷ." Is that good or bad? Can't tell without knowing revenue/COGS. Days of supply (DIO) normalizes for business size and allows meaningful comparison over time and against benchmarks.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Optimize inventory for average demand without planning for peak. If seasonal peak demand is 3× average, and you plan inventory for average, you'll stock out every peak season. Inventory planning must explicitly account for seasonal demand variation — holding more stock pre-peak, drawing down through peak, replenishing after.
