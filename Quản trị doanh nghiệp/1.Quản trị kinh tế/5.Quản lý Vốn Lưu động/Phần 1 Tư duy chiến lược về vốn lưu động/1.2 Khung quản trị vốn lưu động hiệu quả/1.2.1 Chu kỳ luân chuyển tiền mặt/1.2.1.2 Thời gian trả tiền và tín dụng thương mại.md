### Thời Gian Trả Tiền và Tín Dụng Thương Mại

DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) phản ánh mức độ công ty tận dụng tín dụng thương mại từ nhà cung cấp — sử dụng thời gian trả tiền như một nguồn tài trợ vốn lưu động ngắn hạn miễn lãi.

> **Bài học:** Tín dụng thương mại (trade credit) là nguồn tài trợ lớn nhất mà hầu hết SME không nhận ra. Tổng AP của các SME VN có thể bằng 30–50% tổng nợ vay ngân hàng — nhưng không tốn chi phí lãi vay. Quản trị DPO thông minh là tối ưu nguồn vốn này mà không phá vỡ quan hệ chuỗi cung ứng.

---

**DPO và Cash Conversion Cycle:**

```
DPO tăng → CCC giảm → ít vốn cần tài trợ

CCC = DSO + DIO - DPO
  If DPO increases from 30 to 45 days: CCC decreases by 15 days
  Capital freed = COGS / 365 × 15 days
  At COGS = 72 tỷ: freed capital = 72/365 × 15 = 2.96 tỷ
  At cost of capital 12%: annual saving = 355 triệu — without any additional financing

AP Turnover = COGS / AP Balance
  Lower AP Turnover = higher DPO = longer payment cycle = more trade credit used
  Target: match DPO to industry norm or negotiate above it
```

**Tín Dụng Thương Mại — Cơ Chế:**

```
Standard payment terms in VN:
  FMCG distribution: Net 30–45 (moderately competitive)
  Manufacturing inputs: Net 30–60 (depends on supplier power)
  Import goods: LC (Letter of Credit) 30–90 days + transit time
  Services: Net 15–30 (often shorter; no goods as collateral)

Trade Credit as Financing:
  Annual COGS: 72 tỷ
  DPO: 45 days
  Trade credit financing = 72/365 × 45 = 8.88 tỷ (essentially an interest-free loan)
  
  Equivalent bank loan: 8.88 tỷ at 10% = 888 triệu/year in interest saved
  → This "saving" is the economic value of trade credit

Implicit Cost of Trade Credit (when early payment discount is offered):
  Supplier offers 2/10 Net 45 (2% discount if paid in 10 days vs 45 days)
  Cost of NOT taking discount = (2%/98%) × (365/35) = 21.3% annualized
  
  If bank rate = 10% → take the discount (cheaper than bank finance)
  If no credit access → skip discount; use the 35 extra days as financing
```

**Thương Lượng Nâng DPO:**

```
Negotiation Levers for Better Payment Terms:

1. VOLUME COMMITMENT:
   "We commit to 20% volume growth over next year in exchange for Net 60 terms"
   → Supplier gets more revenue; buyer gets longer payment window
   
2. FORECASTING TRANSPARENCY:
   "We'll share 3-month rolling purchase forecast so you can plan production"
   → Supplier benefits from predictability; may extend terms as quid pro quo
   
3. PAYMENT RELIABILITY TRACK RECORD:
   "We've paid on time for 24 consecutive months — request to extend to Net 45"
   → Demonstrated reliability justifies better terms
   
4. EXCLUSIVITY OR PREFERRED SUPPLIER STATUS:
   "We'll make you sole supplier for category X in exchange for Net 60"
   → Supplier reduces sales cost; shares benefit via extended terms
   
5. SUPPLY CHAIN FINANCE:
   Offer SCF arrangement (bank pays supplier immediately; buyer pays bank at Net 60)
   → Supplier gets immediate liquidity; buyer gets extended terms; no relationship cost

Limits:
  Don't push DPO beyond what the supplier can absorb financially
  Tier 1 suppliers with tight cash flow: DPO push → they price it into quotes
  If supplier prices in the cost of extended terms → net benefit is zero
```

**AP Process Efficiency:**

```
Common causes of AP being paid EARLIER than necessary (waste of cash):
  □ No clear policy: team pays when invoice arrives, not when due
  □ Discount confusion: paying early for 0.5% discount that's not worth it
  □ Supplier relationship pressure: "they called asking for early payment"
  □ Lack of cash flow visibility: "we have cash now so let's pay"
  □ Approval bottleneck: invoice approved late → emergency early payment to avoid overdue

Solution:
  Payment runs on fixed schedule (e.g., Tuesdays for Net 30; Fridays for Net 15)
  Never pay before due date without explicit CFO approval + discount calculation
  Automate reminders for payment due dates in accounting system
  
Payment Process Timeline:
  Day 0: Invoice received → entered in system with due date
  Day Due-5: Review for disputes or discrepancies
  Day Due: Process payment in Tuesday/Friday batch run
  Day Due+1: Confirm payment; update AP ledger
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Dell Computer built its competitive advantage in the 1990s partly on working capital superiority. Dell collected from customers (credit cards) before paying suppliers — achieving negative CCC of -35 days at peak. This meant customers were funding Dell's operations. As one analyst observed: "Dell's fastest growing asset was cash." This model — collect before you pay — is the gold standard. For VN businesses: B2C channels (consumer-facing) tend toward shorter DSO than B2B. Companies with mixed channels should strategically grow the B2C/prepaid share to structurally improve CCC.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Treat all trade credit the same. Some AP is cheap (suppliers with excess capacity; willing to carry receivables); some is expensive (supplier factors in cost via higher prices; early payment discount on table). Calculate the effective cost of each AP relationship; optimize accordingly.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Ignore late payment penalties. Many contracts have late payment penalties (0.05–0.1%/day in VN, per Civil Code); some have escalating rates. Paying 35 days late to "extend DPO" when the contract has 0.05%/day penalty = 18.25% annualized penalty rate — far more expensive than the bank loan you were trying to save.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Let AP disputes linger. When an AP invoice is in dispute (wrong amount, quality issue), the payment gets held up — but the relationship deteriorates. Resolve disputes within 5 business days with a clear process: finance holds payment; procurement or quality resolves dispute; once resolved, pay immediately. Don't use disputes as an informal way to extend DPO.
