### Cơ Chế Phân Bổ Vốn Theo Ưu Tiên Chiến Lược

Phân bổ vốn lưu động không phải là kết quả tự nhiên của hoạt động kinh doanh — là quyết định chiến lược chủ động. Khi vốn có hạn, ưu tiên nào được cấp vốn trước? Cơ chế này phải được thiết kế rõ ràng, không phải để từng bộ phận "tranh giành" theo quyền lực nội bộ.

> **Bài học:** Trong điều kiện vốn dồi dào, phân bổ tự nhiên hoạt động được. Trong điều kiện vốn eo hẹp (tháng Tết, khủng hoảng, giai đoạn mở rộng), không có cơ chế phân bổ rõ ràng → quyết định theo cảm tính và quyền lực → nguồn lực chảy sai hướng → chiến lược thất bại dù kế hoạch đúng.

---

**Ưu Tiên Phân Bổ Vốn Lưu Động — Hierarchy:**

```
Tier 1 — Non-negotiable obligations (MUST fund, no exception):
  Payroll (+ BHXH 22.5% employer contribution)
  Tax obligations (VAT monthly; CIT quarterly)
  Bank loan repayments (principal + interest)
  Utility bills (electricity, water — operational continuity)
  → These represent legal obligations and operational survival

Tier 2 — Core operational continuity (SHOULD fund, very limited tradeoff):
  Tier 1 supplier payments (strategic, single-source, long-lead)
  Inventory replenishment for Category A SKUs
  Key customer credit support (customers generating 60%+ revenue)
  → Interruption causes direct revenue loss or operational shutdown

Tier 3 — Growth and efficiency investments (CAN defer if capital constrained):
  New product inventory buildup
  New customer credit extensions
  Category B/C inventory investments
  Working capital for new market entries
  → Deferral delays growth but doesn't threaten core business

Tier 4 — Discretionary (DEFER when constrained):
  Opportunistic inventory purchases ("supplier offered discount")
  Pre-emptive large purchases to "hedge" future price increases
  Non-strategic credit extensions
  → Review after Tier 1–3 are fully funded
```

---

**Capital Allocation Decision Matrix:**

```
When capital is constrained, evaluate each request:

Criteria                | Weight | Score (1–5)
------------------------|--------|---------------------------
Strategic importance    | 30%    | 5=critical to strategy; 1=nice to have
Revenue protection      | 25%    | 5=losing revenue without it; 1=no revenue impact
Time sensitivity        | 25%    | 5=must fund this week; 1=can wait 3 months
Risk of NOT funding     | 20%    | 5=severe consequences; 1=minimal impact

Weighted score → priority ranking → fund in order until capital exhausted

Example allocation decision:
  Request A: Payroll funding (36/40 = 90%) → Fund immediately
  Request B: Tier 1 supplier for A-item inventory (31/40 = 78%) → Fund this week
  Request C: Marketing spend for Q3 campaign (18/40 = 45%) → Defer to Q3
  Request D: Trial inventory for new SKU (12/40 = 30%) → Defer to next quarter
```

**Cash Release Levers — Nguồn Vốn Tình Huống:**

```
When capital is tight, in order of preference:

1. Accelerate AR collections (quickest, no cost):
   □ Call top 10 debtors; collect on overdue invoices
   □ Offer 1% early payment incentive for invoices due in 30+ days
   □ Potential: 2–5 tỷ released within 2 weeks

2. Delay non-critical AP (medium speed, relationship risk):
   □ Request 2-week extension from Tier 2/3 suppliers
   □ Communicate proactively ("cash timing issue; will pay by X")
   □ Potential: 1–3 tỷ preserved 2–4 weeks

3. Liquidate slow-moving inventory (1–4 weeks, margin cost):
   □ Promote or discount Category C inventory
   □ Sell at cost or slight loss to free capital
   □ Potential: 1–4 tỷ released in 2–6 weeks

4. Draw credit line (immediate if available):
   □ Use pre-approved credit facility
   □ Cost: bank interest rate (10–15%/year)
   □ Potential: up to credit limit in 1–2 days

5. Factoring / invoice discounting (3–7 days):
   □ Sell confirmed AR to factoring company
   □ Cost: 2–5% of invoice value
   □ Potential: significant depending on AR quality

6. Emergency equity / shareholder loan (2–4 weeks):
   □ Shareholder bridge loan or emergency equity injection
   □ Last resort; signals financial stress to market
```

**Governance: Monthly Capital Allocation Review:**

```
Meeting: Monthly CFO + Business Unit Heads (30 minutes)

Agenda:
  1. Cash position report: current balance vs minimum threshold
  2. 13-week rolling cash forecast: identify gaps
  3. Capital allocation requests: rank by priority matrix
  4. Decision: approve, defer, or seek additional funding
  5. Follow-up: who owns each action; deadline

Output:
  Approved funding list for next 4 weeks
  Deferred list: items approved but funded next cycle
  Declined list: items rejected with rationale
  
Documentation:
  All allocation decisions documented with rationale
  Track actual vs planned capital deployment monthly
  Learn from variances: adjust matrix weights if priorities were wrong
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Capital allocation is the most important job of the CEO and CFO. Warren Buffett has said this explicitly: "The CEO of a company is the chief capital allocator." This is as true for working capital as for strategic capital. In practice, most SME CEOs focus on operations (sales, delivery, product) and abdicate working capital allocation to the bookkeeper. The result: payroll comes first (obvious), then whoever shouts loudest, then whoever is most persistent. This implicit allocation by persistence is the enemy of strategic capital deployment.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Fund all requests equally when capital is tight ("cut everything by 20%"). Pro-rata cuts are the lazy solution — they equally damage both critical and non-critical activities. A demand-driven, priority-ranked allocation protects the most important activities and absorbs all the cut in the lowest-priority ones. Across-the-board cuts are fair but suboptimal.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Allow departments to "hoard" working capital. "Purchasing bought 3 months of safety stock because they were worried." Now cash is locked in inventory while other departments are starved. Capital hoarding by individual departments destroys company-level efficiency. Central visibility + central allocation authority (CFO) prevents this.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Over-rely on credit line for structural capital needs. "We have a 10 tỷ credit line; just draw it if we're tight." Credit lines are for short-term, self-liquidating needs (buy inventory; sell; collect; repay). Using revolving credit to fund permanent WC growth (or losses) creates a death spiral: line maxed out; bank demands repayment; company has no cash to repay.
