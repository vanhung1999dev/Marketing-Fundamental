### Tiêu Chí Phân Loại Khách Hàng Theo Rủi Ro Tín Dụng

Chính sách tín dụng hiệu quả bắt đầu bằng việc phân loại đúng rủi ro của từng khách hàng — không phải cảm tính dựa trên mối quan hệ, không phải dựa trên quy mô đơn hàng, mà dựa trên khả năng và ý chí thanh toán thực sự.

> **Bài học:** Khách hàng lớn nhất không phải là khách hàng tốt nhất nếu họ luôn trả trễ hoặc có rủi ro vỡ nợ cao. Bán nhiều nhưng không thu được tiền = tặng hàng. Hệ thống phân loại rủi ro tín dụng rõ ràng bảo vệ công ty khỏi sự hấp dẫn ngắn hạn của đơn hàng lớn từ khách hàng rủi ro cao.

---

**Credit Scoring Framework — Tiêu Chí Đánh Giá:**

```
QUANTITATIVE FACTORS (60% weight):

1. Payment History (30%):
  5 points: Always pays on time or early (0–5 days late tolerance)
  4 points: Occasionally 5–15 days late; always pays eventually
  3 points: Regularly 15–30 days late; requires follow-up
  2 points: Sometimes 30–60 days late; requires escalation
  1 point:  Frequently >60 days late; payment uncertain
  
  Source: Internal AR aging history; minimum 6 months of data

2. Financial Strength (20%):
  5 points: Strong financials (audited); liquid; diversified revenue
  4 points: Good financials; adequate liquidity; known stable business
  3 points: Limited financial visibility; owner-operated SME; no financials available
  2 points: Visible financial stress signals (employee complaints, supplier COD)
  1 point:  Known financial difficulty; restructuring; legal actions
  
  Source: Credit bureau (CIC/VCCI data); public information; trade references

3. Business Tenure (10%):
  5 points: In business >10 years; established brand; stable ownership
  4 points: In business 5–10 years; proven track record
  3 points: In business 2–5 years; growing but short history
  2 points: In business 1–2 years; minimal track record
  1 point:  <1 year in business; startup risk
```

```
QUALITATIVE FACTORS (40% weight):

4. Order Volume & Loyalty (20%):
  5 points: Top 10 customer; strategic partner; >3 years relationship
  4 points: Regular customer; consistent volume; 1–3 years relationship
  3 points: Occasional customer; moderate volume
  2 points: Infrequent; small orders; limited dependency
  1 point:  One-time or trial customer; no relationship established

5. Industry and Market Position (10%):
  5 points: Strong market position; growing industry; diversified end-markets
  4 points: Solid market position; stable industry
  3 points: Moderate position; cyclical or competitive industry
  2 points: Weak position; declining industry; high competition
  1 point:  Highly vulnerable (single customer dependency; commodity price exposure)

6. Management Quality & Transparency (10%):
  5 points: Professional management; responsive communication; shares financials willingly
  4 points: Generally professional; communicates proactively
  3 points: Average engagement; answers questions when asked
  2 points: Evasive communication; disputes often; hard to reach
  1 point:  Poor communication; refuses to provide information; past disputes unresolved

Total Score: 5–25 points
```

**Risk Tiers and Credit Policy:**

```
TIER A (Score 20–25): Premium Customer
  Credit limit: Up to 15% of customer's estimated monthly revenue
  Payment terms: Net 45–60 (negotiate to incentivize relationship)
  Monitoring: Annual credit review
  Collection: Standard automated; no special action until 15+ days late
  
TIER B (Score 15–19): Standard Customer
  Credit limit: Up to 10% of customer's estimated monthly revenue
  Payment terms: Net 30–45
  Monitoring: Semi-annual credit review; review if score drops
  Collection: Reminder at Day 0 due; follow-up call at Day +7
  
TIER C (Score 10–14): Caution Customer
  Credit limit: Small limit (equivalent to 1–2 orders)
  Payment terms: Net 15 or 50% deposit + Net 15 on balance
  Monitoring: Quarterly review; automatic downgrade to D if one payment > 30 days late
  Collection: Active collection from Day 0 due; escalate at Day +5
  
TIER D (Score <10): High Risk
  Credit limit: Zero — COD or full prepayment only
  Payment terms: 100% advance payment before shipment
  Monitoring: Annual re-evaluation for tier upgrade
  
New Customers (insufficient history):
  Start at Tier C regardless of apparent size
  Promote to Tier B after 3 on-time payments
  Promote to Tier A after 12 months consistent payment + financial strength confirmation
```

---

**Credit Review Process:**

```
New Customer:
  □ Complete credit application form (business registration + references + financial info)
  □ Credit team scores within 2 business days
  □ Decision communicated to sales within 3 business days
  □ Sales does NOT extend credit until finance approves

Annual Review:
  □ All Tier B–A accounts reviewed annually (or when trigger event occurs)
  □ Updated payment history from AR system
  □ Updated financial info requested (especially for Tier C with limits)
  □ Score recalculated; tier adjusted if needed
  □ Customer notified of any term changes with 30 days notice

Trigger Events (immediate review):
  □ Customer is > 30 days overdue on any invoice
  □ External news: customer in legal dispute, key personnel leaving, major contract loss
  □ Sales team reports customer requesting payment extension (stress signal)
  □ Any bounced check or payment reversal
  
Tier Downgrade:
  Downgrade does NOT require customer consent
  But communicate respectfully: "Due to recent payment experience, we need to adjust terms"
  Provide 30-day transition period (not abrupt switch to COD)
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** The 5 C's of credit (Character, Capacity, Capital, Conditions, Collateral) have been used by bankers for 150 years because they capture the most relevant dimensions of credit risk. Character (intention to pay — payment history, management quality) is the most predictive but hardest to quantify. Capacity (ability to pay — financials, cash flow) is the most objective. For B2B trade credit in VN, Character and Capacity dominate because collateral is rarely taken. The credit scoring framework above essentially operationalizes these two C's.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Base credit tier on order size rather than credit risk. "This customer orders 2 tỷ per month — they must get better terms." Large order size with poor payment history = large uncollected balance. High-volume Tier C customers (big orders, slow payment) should NOT be promoted to Tier B/A. Volume and credit quality are independent — never conflate them.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Never update credit tiers once assigned. A Tier A customer from 3 years ago might have deteriorated significantly (new debt, leadership change, market decline). Annual review catches deterioration before it becomes a bad debt. Conversely, a Tier C customer who has paid on time for 18 months deserves a review for promotion — rewarding good behavior builds loyalty.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Create complex scoring system that nobody uses. 20-criteria credit scorecard requiring 3 financial statements, bank references, and site visit — for a 50 triệu order — won't be used. Calibrate credit review complexity to order size and risk: small orders (<50 triệu) → simplified 3-criteria score; medium orders (50–200 triệu) → standard 6-criteria model; large orders (>200 triệu) → full credit analysis. Make it proportionate.
