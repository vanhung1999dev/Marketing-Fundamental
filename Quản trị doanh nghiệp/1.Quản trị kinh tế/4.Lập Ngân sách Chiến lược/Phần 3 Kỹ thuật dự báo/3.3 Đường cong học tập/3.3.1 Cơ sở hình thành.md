### Cơ Sở Hình Thành Của Đường Cong Học Tập

Đường cong học tập (Learning Curve) mô tả hiện tượng: khi tích lũy kinh nghiệm và khối lượng sản xuất, chi phí đơn vị giảm theo một tỷ lệ có thể dự đoán được. Đây là một trong những insights quan trọng nhất trong quản trị chi phí — nó làm nền tảng cho nhiều quyết định về pricing, bidding, và long-term budgeting.

> **Bài học:** Learning curve không phải lý thuyết trừu tượng — là thực tế có thể quantify và budget cho. Công ty nào hiểu và khai thác learning curve sẽ có lợi thế chi phí có tính tích lũy. Công ty nào không nhận ra nó sẽ over-budget cost cho sản phẩm trưởng thành và under-price contract bid ở giai đoạn đầu.

---

**Phát Hiện Ban Đầu:**

```
Năm 1936, T.P. Wright (kỹ sư hàng không Mỹ) phát hiện:
  Khi số lượng máy bay sản xuất tăng gấp đôi,
  thời gian lao động trực tiếp giảm khoảng 20%

Gọi đây là "80% learning curve"

Ví dụ:
  Unit 1: 1,000 giờ lao động
  Unit 2 (doubled from 1): 800 giờ (80% của unit 1)
  Unit 4 (doubled from 2): 640 giờ (80% của unit 2)
  Unit 8 (doubled from 4): 512 giờ (80% của unit 4)
  Unit 16: 409.6 giờ (80% của unit 8)
```

---

**Cơ Sở Kinh Tế và Tâm Lý Học:**

Learning curve xảy ra do 3 nhóm nguyên nhân:

**1. Labour learning (cá nhân và tập thể):**
- Workers become more skilled at repetitive tasks
- Fine motor movements become automatic; cognitive load drops
- Workers learn shortcuts and better sequencing
- Errors decrease as experience builds
- Example: thợ hàn mới vs thợ hàn 5 năm kinh nghiệm — cùng task, khác tốc độ và chất lượng

**2. Process and method improvements:**
- Better tooling and fixtures discovered through practice
- Bottlenecks identified and eliminated
- Better sequencing of operations
- Standard work procedures developed and refined
- Example: lần đầu setup máy mất 4 giờ → sau 50 lần chỉ mất 45 phút

**3. Technology and design improvements:**
- Product redesign for easier manufacturing (DfM)
- Better raw material specifications
- Equipment and jig improvements
- Example: Apple redesigning iPhone components to be more assembly-friendly with each generation

---

**Experience Curve vs Learning Curve — phân biệt:**

```
LEARNING CURVE (narrow definition):
  → Focuses on direct labour cost reduction
  → Unit of analysis: direct labour hours per unit
  → Driver: repetitive task mastery

EXPERIENCE CURVE (Boston Consulting Group, 1966):
  → Broader concept: total cost per unit decreases with cumulative volume
  → Includes: labour + overhead + materials + capital equipment
  → Drivers: all forms of cost reduction (learning, scale, innovation, standardization)
  → Typical: 20–30% cost reduction each time cumulative volume doubles

BCG Experience Curve: used to explain why cost leaders in mature industries
(high cumulative volume) have structural cost advantages over late entrants
```

---

**Industries và Context Where Learning Curves Are Strong:**

```
High learning curve effect:
  Aerospace and defense manufacturing (Wright's original industry)
  Semiconductor production (Moore's Law is partly experience curve)
  Shipbuilding
  Custom software development (first release expensive; maintenance decreasing)
  Surgery and medical procedures (well-documented in medical literature)
  Call center operations (agents get faster with experience)

Moderate learning curve effect:
  Food manufacturing (consistent production methods)
  Automotive assembly (Toyota, standardized work)
  Service industry (training and standardization)

Low learning curve effect:
  Highly automated production (machine-paced, not labour-paced)
  Pure commodity extraction (mining, drilling)
  Truly one-of-a-kind custom projects (each is unique)

VN business context:
  Textile/garment manufacturing: significant learning curve
  Electronics assembly: strong learning curve
  Construction: moderate (each project has unique elements)
  Software development: strong learning curve for similar product families
```

---

**Strategic Implications of Learning Curve:**

```
1. PRICING STRATEGY:
   Price based on future (low-volume-derived) cost, not current (high-cost) cost
   Accept low/no margin early to build volume → cost drops → margin improves
   This is loss-leader strategy based on learning curve economics
   Tesla's approach: early models expensive; cost decreases with volume

2. MARKET SHARE AND COST LEADERSHIP:
   Company with highest cumulative volume has lowest cost
   → BCG's "get big fast" strategy in growth markets
   → Winning the volume game early creates durable cost advantage

3. BIDDING/CONTRACT PRICING:
   Multi-unit contract: first units expensive; later units cheap
   Bid price = average cost over contract life, not first-unit cost
   → Government contracts (defense, infrastructure) frequently use this logic

4. CAPACITY PLANNING:
   As learning reduces time per unit → capacity increases without new investment
   → Budget for output growth without proportional cost growth
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Intel's manufacturing strategy for decades was explicitly learning-curve-driven. "Copy Exactly!" — Intel's approach to replicating manufacturing processes precisely across fabs — was designed to maximize learning curve benefits: standardize everything, then scale it. Combined with aggressive capacity expansion, this created the cost leadership that made Intel nearly unassailable for two decades. The learning curve is not just a budgeting tool — it's a competitive strategy.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Assume all cost reductions are learning curve effects. Some cost reductions come from lower raw material prices, new technology, or scale economies — not learning. These have different durability and predictability. Always diagnose: is this cost reduction due to learning (durable as long as volume maintained) or commodity cycle (cyclical, will reverse)?

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Apply learning curve to new products without verifying the assumption. If the new product uses completely different technology from historical products, historical learning curve rate may not apply. Validate with first few production runs before projecting forward.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Forget that learning curve requires consistent production. Learning is disrupted by: long production gaps (workers forget); high turnover (new workers restart the learning); design changes (reset the clock on new procedures); outsourcing (manufacturer doesn't accumulate your learning). Budget for learning curve only when production conditions are stable enough to actually realize the learning.
