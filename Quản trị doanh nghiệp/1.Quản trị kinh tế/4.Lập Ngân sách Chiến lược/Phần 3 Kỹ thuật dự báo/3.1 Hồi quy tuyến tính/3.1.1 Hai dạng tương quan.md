### Hai Dạng Tương Quan Trong Hồi Quy Tuyến Tính

Hồi quy tuyến tính (Linear Regression) là công cụ thống kê dự báo giá trị của một biến dựa trên mối quan hệ với một hoặc nhiều biến khác. Để sử dụng hồi quy đúng, phải hiểu hai loại tương quan cơ bản và khi nào chúng có ý nghĩa.

> **Bài học:** Tương quan không phải là nguyên nhân. Trước khi dùng hồi quy để dự báo, hãy hỏi: "Có lý do kinh tế/logic nào giải thích mối quan hệ này không?" Nếu không, correlation có thể là spurious — và mô hình dự báo sẽ fail khi market conditions thay đổi.

---

**Tương quan dương (Positive Correlation):**

```
Hai biến cùng chiều: khi X tăng → Y tăng

Ví dụ kinh doanh:
  GDP tăng → Tiêu dùng tăng
  Số lượng sales rep → Doanh thu (more feet on street → more revenue)
  Marketing spend → New customer acquisition
  Nhiệt độ mùa hè → Doanh số nước uống giải khát

Correlation coefficient (r): 0 < r ≤ +1
  r = +0.9: very strong positive relationship
  r = +0.5: moderate positive relationship
  r = +0.1: weak positive relationship

Scatter plot: points cluster around upward-sloping line
```

---

**Tương quan âm (Negative Correlation):**

```
Hai biến ngược chiều: khi X tăng → Y giảm

Ví dụ kinh doanh:
  Giá sản phẩm tăng → Lượng bán giảm (price elasticity)
  Thời gian giao hàng tăng → Customer satisfaction giảm
  Lãi suất tăng → Đầu tư bất động sản giảm
  Tỷ lệ lạm phát tăng → Tiêu dùng hàng cao cấp giảm

Correlation coefficient (r): -1 ≤ r < 0
  r = -0.9: very strong negative relationship
  r = -0.5: moderate negative relationship
  r = -0.1: weak negative relationship

Scatter plot: points cluster around downward-sloping line
```

---

**Correlation Coefficient (r) — đo lường độ mạnh:**

```
Pearson Correlation r:
  Formula: r = Σ[(Xi - X̄)(Yi - Ȳ)] / [√Σ(Xi - X̄)² × √Σ(Yi - Ȳ)²]

Interpretation:
  |r| = 0.9–1.0: Very strong → high confidence in regression model
  |r| = 0.7–0.9: Strong → useful for forecasting
  |r| = 0.5–0.7: Moderate → use with caution; other factors important
  |r| = 0.3–0.5: Weak → not reliable for forecasting
  |r| = 0.0–0.3: Very weak → relationship not useful

R² (Coefficient of Determination) = r²:
  Measures % of variance in Y explained by X
  R² = 0.81 means 81% of variation in Y is explained by X
  19% is unexplained (other factors or randomness)
```

**Practical Examples cho Budgeting:**

| Y (to forecast) | X (predictor) | Expected r | Use case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Doanh thu | GDP growth (% YoY) | +0.6–0.8 | Top-down macro-linked revenue forecast |
| COGS | Production volume | +0.95+ | Cost of production |
| Sales commissions | Revenue | +0.99 | Variable sales cost |
| Delivery cost | # Orders delivered | +0.85 | Variable logistics cost |
| Electricity cost | Machine hours | +0.80 | Variable overhead |
| Customer churn | Time since last purchase | +0.65 | Churn prediction |

**Non-linear Relationships — khi linear không đủ:**

```
Some relationships are NOT linear:
  
  Diminishing returns: More marketing → more sales, but each additional
    tỷ spent returns less than the previous
    → Use logarithmic or polynomial regression
  
  S-curve (adoption): New product sales follow slow start → rapid growth → plateau
    → Use logistic regression or S-curve model
  
  Seasonal patterns: Sales spike every Tết/Q4
    → Need to decompose trend + seasonality (time series methods)

Always visualize data in scatter plot before choosing regression model.
Linear regression on non-linear data → misleading predictions.
```

---

**Spurious Correlation — khi correlation là ảo:**

```
Classic examples:
  Ice cream sales + drowning deaths (both correlated with summer heat → lurking variable)
  Number of Nicolas Cage movies + pool drownings (random correlation in small dataset)
  Shoe size + reading ability in children (both correlate with age)

Business examples:
  VN: "Companies with more Facebook followers have higher revenue"
    → Both correlate with company size/maturity, not causation
  
  "Stores with more customers complaining have more revenue"
    → More revenue → more customers → more complaints; complaining not causing revenue

Test before using:
  □ Is there a logical causal mechanism?
  □ Does the relationship make economic sense?
  □ Does it hold after controlling for other variables?
  □ Is the data volume sufficient (minimum 20–30 data points for simple regression)?
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** One of the most cited examples of spurious correlation in business is the "Super Bowl Indicator" — the claim that NFC team winning the Super Bowl predicts stock market direction (hit rate ~75% historically). This is purely coincidental correlation with no causal mechanism. In financial forecasting, similar traps exist: models that "worked" in backtesting but fail in live use because the relationship was spurious. The antidote: always require a logical causal mechanism before trusting a correlation for forecasting.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Use r = 0.5 correlation to make confident forecasts. At r = 0.5, only R² = 25% of variation is explained by the predictor. That means 75% of variation comes from other factors. Forecast confidence interval will be very wide. Better to say "revenue is moderately correlated with GDP (r=0.5) — this gives directional guidance but not precision."

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Run regression on time series data without checking for autocorrelation. Monthly sales data is not independent — this month's sales correlates with last month's (trend). Standard regression assumes independence. Violating this assumption → misleadingly small standard errors → overconfident predictions. Time series methods (next section) handle this properly.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Over-fit regression model by adding too many variables. "Revenue correlates with GDP, population, competitor price, weather, social media sentiment..." Adding 10 predictors to a dataset of 30 months will achieve high R² (model fits historical data perfectly) but predicts future poorly. Principle of parsimony: use the fewest variables that explain the relationship adequately.
