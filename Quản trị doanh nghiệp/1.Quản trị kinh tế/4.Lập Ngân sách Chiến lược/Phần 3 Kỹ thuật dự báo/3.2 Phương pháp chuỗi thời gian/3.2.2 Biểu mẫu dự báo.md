### Biểu Mẫu Dự Báo Theo Chuỗi Thời Gian

Phần này trình bày các phương pháp dự báo time series thực tế — từ đơn giản đến phức tạp hơn — với template cụ thể để áp dụng trong lập ngân sách.

> **Bài học:** Simple methods often beat complex ones in business forecasting. Naive forecast ("next year = this year + trend"), moving averages, and exponential smoothing frequently outperform sophisticated models when historical data is limited or noisy. Start simple; add complexity only when simple methods demonstrably fail.

---

**Method 1: Naive Forecast (baseline):**

```
Simplest possible forecast:
  Seasonal Naive: Next period = Same period last year
  
  Example:
    Jan 2025 forecast = Jan 2024 actual = 10.1 tỷ
    Feb 2025 forecast = Feb 2024 actual = 8.9 tỷ
    ...
  
  Improvement: Apply known trend adjustment
    Jan 2025 forecast = Jan 2024 × (1 + expected growth rate)
    = 10.1 × 1.12 (12% growth expected) = 11.3 tỷ
  
  When to use: Short planning horizon; very noisy data; lack of better data
  Accuracy: Often surprisingly competitive vs more complex methods
```

---

**Method 2: Moving Average:**

```
Simple Moving Average (SMA):
  SMA(n) = average of last n periods
  
  3-month SMA for March: (Jan + Feb + Mar actuals) / 3
  
  Use: Smooth out noise; identify underlying trend
  Limitation: Lags; doesn't handle trend well; doesn't handle seasonality

Weighted Moving Average (WMA):
  Assign higher weights to more recent periods
  WMA = 0.5 × last month + 0.3 × 2 months ago + 0.2 × 3 months ago
  
  More responsive to recent changes vs SMA

Centered Moving Average:
  Used in seasonal decomposition (section 3.2.1)
  12-month centered MA to isolate trend from seasonality
```

---

**Method 3: Exponential Smoothing:**

```
Simple Exponential Smoothing (SES):
  Ft+1 = α × At + (1 - α) × Ft
  
  Where:
    Ft+1 = forecast for next period
    At = actual value this period
    Ft = forecast for this period (previous forecast)
    α = smoothing constant (0 < α < 1)
  
  α close to 1: forecast follows recent actuals closely (responsive but volatile)
  α close to 0: forecast changes slowly (stable but slow to adapt)
  
  Example:
    January actual = 10.5 tỷ; January forecast was 10.0 tỷ; α = 0.3
    February forecast = 0.3 × 10.5 + 0.7 × 10.0 = 3.15 + 7.0 = 10.15 tỷ

Holt's Method (handles trend):
  Two smoothing equations: one for level, one for trend
  Appropriate when data has trend but no seasonality

Holt-Winters Method (handles trend + seasonality):
  Three smoothing equations: level + trend + seasonal
  Most appropriate for business revenue forecasting
  Available in Excel as: FORECAST.ETS function (Excel 2016+)
```

---

**Excel FORECAST.ETS — practical tool:**

```
Syntax: =FORECAST.ETS(target_date, values, timeline, [seasonality], [data_completion], [aggregation])

Example:
  Revenue data in column B (monthly, 24 months)
  Dates in column A
  Forecast for next 12 months:
    =FORECAST.ETS(A25, B2:B25, A2:A25, 1, 1, 1)
    → forecasts month 25 with seasonality=1 (automatic detection)

  For confidence interval:
    =FORECAST.ETS.CONFINT(target_date, values, timeline, confidence_level, ...)
    → gives width of confidence interval for each forecast point

  Combined: Point forecast ± CONFINT → gives prediction range

Output: 12 months of forecasts with confidence intervals
  Very practical for budget first-pass; then layer management judgment on top
```

**Forecast Comparison Template:**

```
For budget credibility: compare multiple methods; show range

Month     | Naive   | Moving Avg | Exp Smooth | Average  | Budget
----------|---------|------------|------------|----------|--------
Jan N+1   | 11.3    | 11.1       | 11.5       | 11.3     | 11.5
Feb N+1   | 9.9     | 10.0       | 10.2       | 10.0     | 10.2
Mar N+1   | 9.8     | 9.9        | 10.1       | 9.9      | 10.0
...       | ...     | ...        | ...        | ...      | ...
Annual    | 128.5   | 127.2      | 130.1      | 128.6    | 129.0

Budget = management-adjusted consensus of quantitative forecasts
  If all three methods agree within 5% → high confidence
  If methods diverge significantly → investigate why; seek additional information
```

---

**Tracking Forecast Accuracy:**

```
After each forecast period, track:

MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error):
  MAPE = average of |Actual - Forecast| / Actual × 100%
  
  Interpretation:
    MAPE < 5%: Excellent — for stable, well-understood businesses
    MAPE 5–15%: Good — for typical business with moderate uncertainty
    MAPE > 20%: Poor — model needs improvement or business too unpredictable

Bias check:
  Are errors consistently positive (over-forecast) or negative (under-forecast)?
  Consistent bias means model has structural error → investigate and correct
  
Action:
  Review forecast accuracy quarterly
  If MAPE deteriorating → model needs updating or different method
  If new structural change → rebuild model with more recent data
```

> **Phân tích sâu:** Research by Makridakis et al. (the M-competition studies — rigorous empirical tests of forecasting methods) consistently finds that simple methods (exponential smoothing, naive forecast) often outperform complex statistical methods, especially for short-term forecasts. The reason: simple methods are robust to structural changes; complex models overfit historical patterns that may not repeat. The implication for business: don't always reach for the most sophisticated tool — start simple and use complexity only where it demonstrably helps.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Use point forecast as if it's certain. "Forecast is 128.6 tỷ — that's our budget." But all quantitative forecasts have uncertainty ranges. A 95% CI of 118–140 tỷ means the actual outcome has 95% probability of being in that range. Budget for base case (128.6) but prepare operational contingencies for the downside (118 tỷ).

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Ignore outliers in historical data when building model. COVID-affected months (April–June 2021 in VN: severe lockdowns) are true outliers. If included in model training data, they pull the trend and seasonal estimates down. Treat outliers: either exclude from model and document why, or adjust to normalized values before training.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Build one universal forecast model for all products/channels. Revenue behaves very differently for new products (growth curve) vs mature products (stable); online channel vs offline. Build separate models for each segment where behaviour differs — then aggregate. One-size-fits-all forecast model will be wrong for multiple segments.
