### Xây Dựng Cấu Trúc Đội Ngũ

Cách một sales team được structured — roles, territories, specializations, spans of control — determines what it can and cannot accomplish. Wrong structure limits performance regardless of individual talent; right structure amplifies talent. As company scales from 5 to 50 to 500 in sales, structure evolves — and each transition creates org design challenges that need thoughtful navigation rather than defaulting to how it's always been done or how competitors are structured.

**Sales Role Specialization**: early-stage companies have "full-cycle AEs" who prospect, qualify, demo, và close. As team scales, specialization typically emerges: *SDRs/BDRs* (outbound prospecting and top-of-funnel); *AEs* (manage qualified opportunities through close); *SEs/Pre-sales* (technical demo, proof of concept); *Customer Success/AMs* (post-sale retention and expansion). Specialization increases efficiency (each role optimized for specific task) but increases coordination need. The tradeoff: specialize when volume justifies it; keep full-cycle when coordination cost exceeds specialization benefit.

**Territory Design**: how you divide customers/prospects among reps dramatically affects who can hit quota and who cannot. Bad territory design creates structural winners (reps with great territories who'd succeed despite average skill) và structural losers (reps with poor territories who'd struggle despite excellent skill). Territory design principles: *Balance potential* — roughly equal revenue potential across territories; *Geographic logic* — minimize travel time; *Account history* — legacy relationships follow reps; *Segment consistency* — rep specializes in one segment type, not mixed. Reassign territories when market changes make them unbalanced.

**Manager Span of Control**: how many reps per manager determines coaching quality. Too many (10+ per manager): manager spends time on administrative tasks, can't coach effectively, deals with fires rather than developing people. Too few (2-3 per manager): expensive overhead ratio. Optimal range for sales typically 6-8 reps per manager for complex enterprise (where deals need more coaching involvement) to 8-12 for transactional inside sales (where reps are more independent). Manager's primary job is coaching and pipeline development — structure should enable this.

**Overlaying Functional Support**: beyond core sales roles, who provides supporting functions? Marketing (demand generation); Revenue Operations (data, tools, process); Enablement (training, playbooks, content); Deal Desk (complex pricing approval). Understanding which of these your team has access to versus needs to build determines realistic sales capacity per rep. A team without RevOps or Enablement functions places more administrative burden on salespeople và managers, reducing selling time.

> **Bài học:** Annual "Structure Review" — ask: "Is our current structure the best match for our current go-to-market strategy, customer profile, and team size?" Structure that made sense at $5M ARR may constrain growth at $50M ARR. Common triggers for structure review: rapid headcount growth, new product lines requiring different selling motion, geographic expansion, segment shift (moving upmarket or downmarket), and significant talent turnover that reveals structural issues.

> **Phân tích sâu:** McKinsey research on sales force design shows: getting the right sales structure can deliver 5-10% revenue uplift without any increase in headcount or compensation — purely through better alignment of resources to opportunity. Territory inequity, in particular, is systematically underestimated: studies show that 30-40% of quota attainment variance across reps is explained by territory quality, not rep skill. Fixing territory inequity alone materially improves both performance and rep satisfaction.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Structuring around existing people rather than optimal design. "We structured this team around what our current 3 reps are good at" — instead of "what does optimal structure look like, and how do we close gap?" Structure should be designed for optimal performance, then staffed; not designed around current staff's preferences or capabilities.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Over-specializing too early. Specialization creates coordination overhead and career path complexity. A 3-person sales team with SDR, AE, and AM roles has more coordination cost than benefit versus 3 full-cycle AEs. Specialize when volume justifies it — typically when same task is being done by multiple people at high enough frequency to justify dedicated specialist.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Reorganizing too frequently in response to each strategic shift. Reorgs are disruptive: relationships break, momentum is lost, people spend weeks in uncertainty instead of selling. Each reorg has real performance cost for 3-6 months of transition. Before restructuring, ask: "What specific problem will this solve? Could we solve it without structural change? Is the benefit worth the transition cost?" Bias towards stability when possible; restructure when structural issues are materially limiting performance.
