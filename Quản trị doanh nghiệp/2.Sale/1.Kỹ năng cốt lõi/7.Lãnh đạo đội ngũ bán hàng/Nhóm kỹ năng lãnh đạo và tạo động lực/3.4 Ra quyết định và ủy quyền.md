### Ra Quyết Định Và Ủy Quyền

Sales managers phải ra quyết định liên tục — từ chiến thuật nhỏ (nên send proposal cho deal này không?) đến chiến lược lớn (nên tập trung vào segment nào năm nay?). Đồng thời, effective manager không ra tất cả quyết định một mình — họ biết ủy quyền (delegate) một cách thoughtful, vừa release bandwidth cho mình vừa develop decision-making capability trong team. The tension between "making good decisions" and "developing others to make decisions" is one of the central challenges of management.

**Decision Quality vs. Decision Speed**: không phải tất cả quyết định đều cần same level of deliberation. Framework: *High-stakes, reversible decisions* — take more time, gather more information, involve more perspectives; *High-stakes, irreversible decisions* — take most time, extremely careful; *Low-stakes, reversible decisions* — decide quickly, often delegate; *Low-stakes, irreversible* — fast but careful. Misallocating deliberation effort is costly: too much analysis on minor decisions kills pace; too little on major decisions creates risk. Categorize before you analyze.

**Creating Decision Clarity with Team**: confusion about who makes which decisions is a major source of organizational friction. Use DACI framework: *Driver* (who is responsible for making decision happen?), *Approver* (who has final say?), *Contributors* (who provides input?), *Informed* (who needs to know the outcome?). For recurring decision types — deal discount approval, territory exception, resource request — define DACI explicitly so same question doesn't escalate every time. Clear decision rights reduce manager as bottleneck.

**Effective Delegation**: delegation is not abandonment — it's transfer of decision authority với appropriate support. The delegation spectrum: *Tell* (I'll decide, please implement); *Sell* (I've decided, here's why, do you have concerns?); *Consult* (I want your input before I decide); *Agree* (we'll decide together); *Delegate* (you decide, keep me informed); *Abdicate* (you're on your own). Match delegation level to rep's demonstrated capability and stakes of decision. Over-delegate too early → unready rep makes poor decision; under-delegate always → manager bottleneck and rep doesn't develop.

**Learning from Decisions**: building decision-making culture requires treating past decisions as learning opportunities. Post-decision review (not blame session): "What did we decide? What did we expect would happen? What actually happened? Why the gap? What would we do differently?" This learning loop improves future decision quality and builds team's collective judgment. When decisions are made and forgotten without debrief, same mistakes recur; when they're reviewed systematically, team gets smarter with each cycle.

> **Bài học:** Identify decisions you're currently making that a senior rep or team lead could make with appropriate guidance. Delegate one category of decisions monthly, với explicit support: "You're now empowered to approve discounts up to 10% without my approval. Here's the criteria I use when I make these calls. Make the decision and share outcome with me so I can give you feedback." This builds rep decision-making capability and frees you for higher-leverage activities — while maintaining oversight through feedback rather than pre-approval.

> **Phân tích sâu:** Kleiner Perkins research on high-growth company management shows: one of the most common failure modes for sales leaders who scale from small team to large team is failure to shift from doing to enabling. Leaders who built team from 0-10 reps through personal involvement in every deal try to continue same approach at 50 reps — creating bottleneck that limits team scalability and burns out leader. The transition from "best individual salesperson who manages" to "leader who develops other leaders" requires deliberate choice to delegate decision authority.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Making decisions too slowly out of fear of being wrong. "Analysis paralysis" in sales management — waiting for perfect information before deciding — creates organizational lag that kills momentum. In business, decisions made with 70% information made quickly outperform decisions made with 90% information made slowly, because pace matters. Get comfortable with informed uncertainty.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Reversing delegates' decisions without clear reason. When manager overrides rep's delegated decision without explanation, rep learns: "My decisions don't actually matter." If you disagree with how rep used delegated authority, coach them — explain your reasoning and explore theirs — rather than simply overriding. The exception: when decision violates ethics, clear policy, or creates significant harm — then override and explain.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Delegating tasks but not authority. "You're responsible for this account" but then manager overrides rep's decisions on that account whenever they disagree. Fake delegation is more demoralizing than no delegation — rep has accountability without authority. True delegation requires accepting that delegate will make some decisions differently than you would, and that's OK (sometimes better, sometimes worse, but development requires real stakes).
