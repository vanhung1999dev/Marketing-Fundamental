### Phối Hợp Với Lãnh Đạo Cấp Cao

Sales manager không phải isolated individual contributor quản lý một team — họ là critical link trong organizational hierarchy, translating company strategy downward into team execution và translating ground-level reality upward into strategic intelligence. Ability to work effectively với senior leadership — providing accurate information, representing team fairly, influencing decisions that affect team — is essential skill that separates managers who advance from managers who plateau.

**Managing Up Effectively**: "managing up" không phải politicking — đó là skill của communication với senior leaders in ways that are useful to them và advance legitimate interests of your team và customers. Principles: *Be concise* — senior leaders are time-constrained; one-page summaries beat 20-slide decks; *Lead with so-what* — "Here's the situation, here's what I recommend, here's why" not "here's 15 minutes of context before I get to my point"; *Bring solutions not just problems* — "We have a territory coverage gap in Northern region — here's my proposed approach" is more valued than "We have a coverage problem"; *Be accurate* — leaders who discover you've been optimistic about your team's performance lose trust permanently.

**Forecast Integrity with Senior Leadership**: perhaps the most trust-defining behavior in relationship with senior leadership is forecast accuracy and candor. Organizations suffer when managers systematically over-forecast to look good, then miss quarter. The manager who consistently provides accurate forecasts — even when forecast is disappointing — builds credibility that enables strategic resource allocation. Senior leaders can make better decisions when working with accurate information; happy talk that turns into miss costs more than honest bad news shared early.

**Advocating for Your Team**: one of manager's most important jobs is advocating upward for what team needs — headcount, tools, territory adjustments, pricing authority, product roadmap influence. Effective advocacy: *Quantify the business case* ("If we add 2 reps in Q3, based on ramp assumptions and territory potential, we can generate additional $1.2M ARR by year end"); *Anticipate objections* (what will leadership's concerns be? Address proactively); *Prioritize asks* (you can't advocate for everything; choose battles that matter most to team performance); *Accept decisions gracefully* (if leadership says no, understand why and either accept or come back with stronger case — don't sulk).

**Translating Strategy to Execution**: when company launches new strategy ("we're moving upmarket," "we're focusing on APAC," "new product becomes primary motion"), sales manager's job is to translate abstract strategy into concrete daily activities for team. What does "moving upmarket" actually mean for: who each rep calls, how they qualify, what demo they run, which metrics they track? Strategy that stays at strategic level without execution translation stays aspirational. Manager bridges this gap.

> **Bài học:** Trước bất kỳ significant meeting với senior leadership nào, prepare by answering: "What decision am I asking them to make? What information do they need? What's my recommended answer? What questions will they likely ask?" This preparation transforms reactive reports into proactive leadership conversations. Leaders notice and remember managers who come prepared with recommendations, not just summaries.

> **Phân tích sâu:** Research on organizational effectiveness by Jim Collins ("Good to Great") highlights: companies that achieve sustained excellent performance have leaders at all levels who are rigorous and honest about reality — not optimists who paper over bad news. In sales, the "brutal facts" principle means: report accurate forecast even when disappointing; surface territory or product problems even when they're uncomfortable; acknowledge when strategic initiatives aren't gaining traction. Organizations that maintain fiction about performance can't correct course in time.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Filtering bad news before it reaches senior leadership. "I don't want to bother them with every problem" → senior leadership makes decisions based on incomplete picture → surprise at quarter end. Better: establish regular briefings where you share full picture — highlights and challenges — with appropriate frequency. Leaders who are surprised by negative outcomes lose confidence in managers who didn't surface problems earlier.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Going around management hierarchy to solve problems — calling CEO directly, or building relationships that bypass your VP to get resources. While understandable when stuck, this creates political problems that outlast the specific resource you were trying to get. Build relationship with your direct manager first; they are your primary channel upward. If that channel is broken, that's the problem to fix.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Over-promising to leadership under pressure. "Q3 is tough but I'm committed to 100%" when pipeline suggests 75% is realistic puts manager in impossible position — either miss committed number or damage team by doing whatever it takes to hit it (discounting irrationally, pulling in Q4 deals, burning customer relationships). Better to commit to what you can honestly deliver and over-perform than to commit beyond capability and under-deliver.
