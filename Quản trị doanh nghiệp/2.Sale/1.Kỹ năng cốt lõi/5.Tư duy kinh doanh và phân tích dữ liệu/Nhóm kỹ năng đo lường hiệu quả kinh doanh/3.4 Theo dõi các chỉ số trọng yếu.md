### Theo Dõi Các Chỉ Số Trọng Yếu

Xây dựng thói quen theo dõi consistent những metrics quan trọng nhất — không phải theo kiểu "nhìn vào khi cần" mà theo lịch cố định với analytical rigor — là điều cho phép phát hiện problems sớm và opportunities nhanh hơn competition. "Monitor what matters, regularly, with clear response protocols" là operating principle của high-performing organizations.

**Mission-Critical Metrics cho Sales**: mỗi organization cần define tập riêng của mình, nhưng universal candidates: (1) *Pipeline health* — coverage ratio (pipeline value / target), stage distribution, age of deals by stage; (2) *Conversion rates* — MQL→SQL, SQL→Opportunity, Opportunity→Proposal, Proposal→Close; (3) *Revenue momentum* — monthly recurring revenue, new ARR, expansion ARR, churn ARR; (4) *Customer health* — NPS trend, product usage, support ticket volume, renewal rate; (5) *Sales productivity* — revenue per rep, quota attainment %, activities per deal won.

**Monitoring Cadence**: different metrics need different monitoring frequencies. *Daily* — activity metrics (calls, emails, meetings) for real-time management; *Weekly* — pipeline health, conversion rates, revenue pacing; *Monthly* — customer satisfaction, churn signals, team performance; *Quarterly* — strategic metrics, year-over-year trends, benchmark comparisons. Over-monitoring creates noise; under-monitoring creates blind spots.

**Threshold Alerts**: không chỉ track metrics — define thresholds that trigger action: "If weekly pipeline add drops below $X, sales manager must investigate and report." "If NPS score drops below Y for 2 consecutive months, customer success team escalates to leadership." Threshold-based alerts convert passive monitoring into active management.

**Trend vs Point-in-Time**: never evaluate a metric in isolation — always in trend context. Single data point tells you where you are; trend tells you where you're going. "Revenue is $5M this month" — is that good or bad? Without knowing if last month was $4.5M (improving) or $5.5M (declining), you can't evaluate. Trend is the story; point-in-time is a chapter.

> **Bài học:** Create "Monthly Business Review Packet" — consistent format with same metrics every month so you can track trends over time. Changing what you measure every month means you never have trend data. Consistency in what you measure is more valuable than perfection in what you choose to measure — you can always adjust later, but can't recreate historical baseline you didn't track.

> **Phân tích sâu:** Toyota's "Andon cord" concept — production line workers can pull a cord to stop the entire production line when they detect a quality issue — is a metaphor for monitoring with action protocols. The power isn't in the monitoring; it's in the response system. Metrics without clear escalation và response protocols are decorative, not operational.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #1:** Tracking too many metrics to track any of them well. The classic "we have 47 metrics on our dashboard" situation where nothing gets proper attention. Ruthless prioritization — 5-7 mission-critical metrics with clear owners và response protocols — outperforms comprehensive metric libraries that no one actively uses.

> **Sai lầm phổ biến #2:** Tracking without acting. Metrics should inform decisions và drive behaviors. If a metric consistently shows yellow or red but no action is taken, the metric is serving no purpose và should be removed or replaced with one that will drive action.

> **Cạm bẫy:** Gaming metrics once they're tracked — "now that we track X, team optimizes for X at expense of unmeasured outcomes." Solution: measure output metrics (deals won, revenue) alongside input metrics (activities) so gaming inputs without delivering outputs is visible. Pairs of metrics that are harder to simultaneously game are more robust.
